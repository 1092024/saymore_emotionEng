Happy Ink Ball


Brushstroke Representation: Feibai
Ancient Chinese ink painters often depicted objects symbolizing vitality and beauty with Feibai brushstrokes, such as vibrant flowers and plants, bright sunlight, or floating flower petals. Through the use of light brushwork, cheerful figures, as well as elements of lines, ink application, and composition in painting, they constructed a pleasant and relaxed atmosphere, demonstrating the literati's expression of the beauty and joy of life

Angry Ink Ball


Brushstroke Representation: Dry Rubbing
Ancient Chinese ink painters often depicted scenes of raging storms, thunder and lightning, and intense anger with dry rubbing brushstrokes, expressing intense emotions of anger, symbolizing the eruption of power and emotional excitement. Through chaotic brushwork, strong contrasts, and a sense of chaos in the composition, they created an atmosphere of excitement and violence, presenting a unique way for literati to express their surging anger and emotional release.

Sad Ink Ball


Brushstroke Representation: Rendering
Ancient Chinese ink painters often used rendering brushstrokes to depict elements such as withered trees, falling flowers, and waning moons, expressing a melancholic emotion. These objects symbolize the withering of life and the passage of time, conveying a deep sense of sorrow through elements such as broken objects and solitary figures in their paintings. The use of lines, ink shades, and composition design collectively create a desolate and sorrowful atmosphere, reflecting the unique way in which ancient literati artists expressed emotions and pursued artistic conceptions.

Fear Ink Ball


Brushstroke Representation: Broken Pen
In ancient Chinese painting, artists often used broken pen strokes to depict shattered objects, cracked rocks, and other elements to express feelings of fear. These broken objects symbolize the fragility and insecurity of life. Through the artist's skillful depiction of lines and ink application, they create an atmosphere of fear and unease. The fragmentation and cracks in the paintings reflect the expression of fear by ancient literati and their profound contemplation of the fragility of life.
